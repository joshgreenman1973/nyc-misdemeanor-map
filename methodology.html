Methodology & sources

How this map was built

This document records every data source, every analytical choice and every known limitation behind the map of New York City misdemeanors and violations. Nothing here is a black box. The two scripts that produce the data are public and re-runnable.

What the map shows

Every record New York City's Police Department classifies as a misdemeanor or a violation — the two lowest tiers of offense, below felonies — from January 1, 2015 through March 31, 2026, aggregated to the city's 78 police precincts. Felonies are excluded entirely. Two separate lenses are offered:

  • Complaints — incidents reported to or recorded by police (the standard "crime" dataset). Measures reported crime.
  • Arrests — arrests made by police. Measures enforcement activity. An arrest is itself an enforcement action, so this lens responds directly to "enforcement pushes."

Dates

Complaints are dated by report date (RPT_DT), the date the incident was reported to police — not the date the offense is alleged to have occurred (CMPLT_FR_DT), which can be years earlier for some offenses. Arrests are dated by arrest date (ARREST_DATE).

2026 is a partial year. It covers the first quarter only (January 1 – March 31). On every chart the line to 2026 is drawn dashed, and 2026 totals should be compared against prior first quarters, not against full years. Year-over-year change figures are suppressed for 2026.

The enforcement-sensitivity classification

The central analytical move is to separate offenses whose recorded counts move with policing strategy from those that mostly reflect underlying victimization. Each offense type is assigned to one of three groups. This is a documented editorial judgment, not an official NYPD category — the complete assignment is exported to classification.csv so it can be inspected or contested.

Enforcement-sensitive Discretionary, officer-initiated offenses. A police officer largely decides whether these become a recorded incident, so the counts rise and fall with proactive enforcement: dangerous drugs, cannabis offenses, criminal trespass, theft of services (fare evasion), gambling, loitering, prostitution, disorderly conduct, weapon possession, intoxicated/impaired driving, vehicle-and-traffic-law offenses, alcoholic-beverage offenses, fraudulent accosting, jostling, burglar's tools, fortune telling.

Complaint-driven Offenses typically reported by a victim or witness, where a member of the public, not an officer, usually initiates the record: petit larceny, assault 3, harassment, criminal mischief, sex crimes, forcible touching, fraud, unauthorized use of a vehicle, possession of stolen property, offenses against children, endangering welfare.

Other / mixed Administrative catch-alls, ambiguous grab-bag categories, and offenses that arise downstream of police contact rather than from a push to police a behavior: offenses against public administration (resisting arrest, obstructing governmental administration), "offense against public order/sensibility," administrative code, miscellaneous penal law, other state laws, anticipatory offenses, escape, agriculture & markets law, health code, uncoded entries.

Why some judgment calls went the way they did

  • "Offenses against public administration" (resisting arrest, obstruction) is placed in Other, not Enforcement-sensitive. Its counts are driven by police contact, but it is a consequence of enforcement rather than a target of an enforcement push, so grouping it with drugs or trespass would overstate the enforcement-sensitive total.
  • "Offense against public order/sensibility" is a large, heterogeneous bucket (public lewdness, unlawful surveillance, and more) that is part proactive and part victim-reported. Rather than guess, it is left in Other.
  • Dangerous weapons (possession) and vehicle-and-traffic-law offenses are treated as enforcement-sensitive because, at the misdemeanor/violation level, they are overwhelmingly discovered through stops and proactive checks.

The most important limitation: this is not all enforcement

For the lowest-level offenses, much enforcement never appears in either dataset here. Fare evasion, open-container, public urination, disorderly conduct and similar quality-of-life matters are frequently resolved with a criminal court summons or a civil/OATH summons, neither of which is a complaint or an arrest. That is why "theft of services" shows only a few thousand complaints across eleven years even though fare evasion enforcement runs far higher. The enforcement-sensitive arrest lens captures the share handled by arrest, but the summons channel is out of scope. A future version could add the NYPD criminal-court-summons dataset.

More generally: complaint data measures reported crime, which is shaped by public willingness to report; arrest data measures police activity, not the amount of crime occurring. Neither is a direct measure of how much low-level offending actually happens.

Geographic method

  • Incidents are aggregated to the precinct of occurrence (ADDR_PCT_CD for complaints, ARREST_PRECINCT for arrests). Records with no precinct are dropped.
  • Precinct 116 (operational since 2024 in southeast Queens) is shown on its own polygon. Precinct code 483 appears on three stray records and is dropped as invalid.
  • The map is not population-normalized. Precinct-level population is not part of these datasets, so counts are raw incidents, not rates per resident. A precinct with more people, more commercial activity or more transit will tend to show higher counts. Compare the enforcement-sensitive share view, which is a within-precinct ratio and so is less sensitive to raw volume.

Data cleaning

  • Offense names are canonicalized: the source data contains truncated and variant spellings of the same offense (for example CRIMINAL MISCHIEF & RELATED OF and CRIMINAL MISCHIEF & RELATED OFFENSES), which are merged to one label.
  • Offense level is read from LAW_CAT_CD (MISDEMEANOR/VIOLATION in complaints; M/V in arrests). Felony, infraction and unclassified levels are excluded.
  • All aggregation is done server-side through the Socrata API (GROUP BY queries); no raw person-level records are stored or shipped.

Reproduce it

Two scripts regenerate everything:

  • fetch_data.py — pulls precinct × year × offense × level counts from the four NYPD datasets.
  • build_data.py — canonicalizes offenses, applies the classification, simplifies the precinct boundaries, and writes data.json, precincts.json and classification.csv.

Run python3 fetch_data.py && python3 build_data.py. No API key is required.
